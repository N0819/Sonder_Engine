## Turn 2  *(162s)*

**Vale:** I let the silence stretch a beat, then give a thin, unhurried smile. "A breakthrough forever on the verge, and a Prime Directive held up like a shield — gentlemen, you've each handed me half a picture and called it a briefing." I step to the tactical rail, uninvited, and study the Array as though I already own the view. "Doctor, you want to wake it. Captain, you want to admire it from a respectful distance. Neither of you has said the quiet part aloud: nobody in this room actually knows what's on the other side of that door." I turn, unbothered by the pips in the room. "So I'll be blunt, since apparently no one else here will. Dr. Vorne — humor me. If your beautiful key were left by people who locked this thing and walked away on purpose, would you still call turning it a breakthrough? Or is that the one word your whole career can't afford to hear?"

The silence settles. I let it sit, let the hum of the bridge fill the space between Vorne's words and mine. Then I smile — thin, unhurried — and deliver the line cleanly, watching it land on each face in turn. Picard's expression doesn't shift. Riker's weight settles differently. Troi watches. I step to the tactical rail, uninvited, and turn my back on them just long enough to study the Array's slow pulse on the viewer — a blue-white rhythm I don't yet own but intend to. Then I turn back, meeting Vorne's eyes, and lay out what neither of them has said. The words come flat, deliberate, letting each one sit. Vorne holds my gaze. His hands still for half a beat. When he speaks, his voice is measured, firm: "Commander Vale, a fair question. But the evidence suggests otherwise. The pulse is not a random emission — it is a structured sequence, a mathematical language. The Kelvans were capable of leaving a warning if they chose. What they left instead is a key. To mistake a key for a lock is to let fear eclipse discovery." He draws a slow breath, and the bridge hums on.

## Turn 3  *(93s)*

**Vale:** "I've had enough philosophy for one afternoon." I don't look at Picard; I look past him, to the ops station. "Mr. Data — run me an active scan of that sequence. Full spectrum, decode what you can, route it to my station." Only then do I turn back to the captain, almost as an afterthought. "Your caution is noted for the record, Captain. But an ethics review that only watches is tourism, and I did not come this far to sightsee. The Doctor says it's a key. Fine. I'd like to see the shape of the lock before anyone in a red uniform decides whether to turn it."

I let the dismissal hang, then shift my gaze past Picard to the ops station—empty, dark—and speak to the air. My voice carries, flat, the order clipped. Data's absence is a silence I don't pause on. When I turn back, Picard's eyes haven't moved from mine. I meet them, hold them, and deliver the rest without heat, without edge—just the weight of a point that needs landing. The word 'tourism' sits in the air between us. Geordi looks up from the engineering console, his hands still. The bridge hums. Picard holds my gaze a beat longer. Then he inclines his head, a fraction of an inch, and says nothing.
